    • Scope: major
    • Script: Anglo-Saxon Insular minuscule
    • Description: This scribe copied fols 1-170. ' are probably in one hand, but there is some change in style after the breaks at fols 64, 154. The writing is skilful, small but clear, fluent and well-spaced: insular g is used for the spirant and caroline g for the stop: s is caroline, but sometimes it descends below the line, especially when doubled and before t : d is rounded, of the same shape and size as eth: a, e, f, h, r are caroline: a diagonal hairline is attached to the base of the caroline and insular forms g : the ends of descenders, except p, turn to the left ... Latin words d is usually upright'. The scribe uses a type of Anglo-Saxon Insular minuscle characteristic of the mid second half of the twelfth century. The aspect is slightly backward-leaning; the ascenders are slightly wedged. The descenders are either tapered or curve on the left. The hand does not extend too much either below or above the line of writing. The nib of the pen is cut in a squarish proportion to give to the script a squarish, but irregular aspect. The scribe mixes both caroline and insular features. The appearance of the ductus is influenced by the gothic style, see the tendency towards diamond shaped lobes and the use of 'st' ligature in particular.
    • Summary of the characteristics of the hand: Summary of the characteristics of the hand. Caroline a with a small lobe, which is not always closed, and pronounced head broken stroke of squarish proportion; a diamon shaped lobe of the b which alternates a more rounded bowl, wedged straight ascender, whose end turns slightly to the left; a one broken stroke c starting with a thicker stroke -- dot-like; d has a thick left-leaning back stroke, occasionally tapered backward on the right, and a small rounded lobe; its head can also be of squarish proportions and the length of the ascender varies. Small closed e with a little tongue on the right side of the squarish shaped head. Three forms of f: a Caroline f, with the shaft sitting on the line and a tall ascender which occasionally curls into itself forming a closed stroke, which alternates tailed f, with a one stroke long descender finishing with an otiose line towards the left beyond the line of writing, and an insular form of f with a tapered descender and a wedged ascender. There are also three forms of g. A Caroline gwith a rounded lobe, which does not always close, but finishes with a small tongue on the top right side of the lobe. The descender curves in a sharp, but rounded, ninety degree angle towards the left, stopping just past the line of the starting point of the lobe. This is a distinct feature of the gothic script. The scribe also uses an insular form of g which has a s shaped descender which curves towards the right just after the writing line, and an open lobe on the right topped by a flat horizontal stroke. There is also a long and thin insular g with a tail curling towards right and a flat head stroke. Caroline h with a thick wedged ascender and a right limb which may or may not curl under the line. The minims of i, m, n and u are traced singularly, irregular in shape and their feet may finish with a small serif or with a sharper angle to the right. Tall wedged ascender l, with the shaft finishing on the line with a small feet curling to the right. Rounded closed o. p and ƿ are mainly distinguishable through meaning. However, p has a descender whose foot curl towards the right and a closed lobe of squarish proportions which closes past the shaft on the left. ƿ is formed with a tailed descender generally curving towards the left and a closed elongated lobe. Insular r with a well defined bow and a descender which curves on the left. Caroline and long tailed r also appear. Caroline s with a flat head-stroke alternates a long s and an insular form with a tailed descender curving to the left. t with straight head stroke and straight shaft which curls to the right. x exaggerated descender curving right. y is always dotted and the descender ends with a flick on the right. æ formed by an insular shape-like a and an e, which sits on shoulder of the a and terminates with a tail on the top right, at times, it sits on a protruding headstroke of the a. ð has pointed lobe, wedged ascender and tailed descender to the left. þ can be formed by a broken stroke forty-five degree ascender and a median stroke with ends on the right with a serif curving on the right again. Other features include: a two strokes D which has a upright stem and a wide lobe closing at the top in a narrow parallel stroke to the stem. and capitals are often, but not regularly, used after the Punctus. Pen ink work is occasionally used to decorate Litterae Notabiliores and in particular the descender of g and g and þ may develop in ornate leaves or floreal motives.

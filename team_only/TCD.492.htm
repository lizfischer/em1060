Bede's Death Song

Date: s. xiimed

Summary:

The manuscript has four items, mainly in Latin: Bede, 'Historia Ecclesiastica Gentis Anglorum' (fols 1r/1-175v/5a), Cuthbert, 'Epistola De Obitu Bedae' (fols175v/6a-177r/4b), a short tract on the progress of Christianity in England (fols 177r/6b-178v/9b) and 'Notationes De Sanctis qui in Anglia Requiescunt' (fols 178v/11b-180r/30a). Bede's Death Song is contained in Cuthbert, 'Epistola De Obitu Bedae', fol. 176r/1a-7a. Dobbie considered that this text belonged to his 'Digby group' lacking 'heonengange hwæt his' from lines 3-4 (pp. 76-83).

There are annotations in English of s. xii2. Lucas lists the following items:

  • fol. 4r 'weoloc scælle'
  • fol. 4r 'seolas',
  • fol. 4r 'octo hund mile long & twa hund mile brad',
  • fol. 4v 'weolocas',
  • fol. 8v 'munuc',
  • fol. 9v 'half mile amurg'.

Physical Description:

Object Description:

    Fols ii + 180 + ii. All parchment except for paper endleaves. All the quires consist of 8 leaves except for the final quire 23, which consists of 4 leaves. According to Lucas, ruling is in crayon, and all quires are HFHF, except the final quire, which is HF' (p. 25).

    Foliation and/or Pagination: Foliation (1958) in pencil excluding paper endleaves.

    Catchwords are partially visible at the end of Quires VIII (f. 64v) and X (f. 80v). Whether they are in the hand of the mains scribe or not is unknown.

Hand Description:

    Number of hands: 2

    Hand: Main
    • Scope: Major
    • Description: The Bede's Death Song is in the main hand, who copied the whole of the main text. The hand is 'upright, fine and regular' (McLachlan, p. 262). McLachlan identified this hand with that of the first hand in Cambridge, Pembroke College 72, vol. 1 (p. 304). Ker says that Old English is 'distinguished in script from Latin only by the use of þ and Þ' (Ker, p. 144).
    Hand: Annotations
    • Scope: Minor
    • Description: There are also annotations in English of s. xii2.

Decoration Description:

    • Illuminated initials in green, red, blue and gold.
    • Incipits and explicits; chapter numbers; names of authorities in red (sometimes silvered).
    • Capitals in blue, red (sometimes silvered), and green, some flourished.

    See McLachlan, Alexander, Lucas.

Binding Description:

    Blind-tooled red/brown leather ca. 1830; rebacked 1947.

History:

    Origin:

    The manuscript belonged to the Benedictine abbey of St. Edmund at Bury St. Edmunds, Suffolk (Ker). Lucas says that it is 'very possible' that the manuscript was written there too (p. 24).

    Provenance:

    The provenance is unknown. Some suggest that the manuscript belonged to John Dee (1528-1608), but there is no proof of his ownership (Lucas, p. 24).

    Acquisition:

    The manuscript came to Trinity College, Dublin, via James Ussher (1581-1656), archbishop of Armagh.

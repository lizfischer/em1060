Gospels

Date: "s.xii 2"

Summary:

A copy of the Gospels in English. Royal 1 A. xiv is textually related to Hatton 38 either by being its examplar or by sharing a common copy of the text. Both manuscripts relate textually to Bodley 441. For an updated discussion of the textual tradition of the Old English Gospell, seeLiuzza 1994 , pp. xliii-lxxiii.

Hand description:

    Number of hands: 2
    Summary: One main hand copied the complete manuscript. However, a twelf-century hand completed the gospels on fols. 32r, 133v and 173v. there may be one more xiii hand fol. 148 (See: Ker 1957, p. 316; Liuzza 1994 , pp. xxxviii-xli) [CHECK ALL THIS AGAINST THE MANUSCRIPT] .
    Hand: 1
    • Scope: major
    • Script: Anglo-Saxon Insular minuscle
    • Description: The principal characteristics of the Royal 1 A. xiv scribe are follows. The general aspect is upright, somewhat uneven in detail, rather elongated laterally with large bowls and relatively short ascenders and descenders, giving the overall page a rounded look, despite, on closer analysis, the rather fractured nature of graphs. Word-spacing and interlinear spacing is generous. Descenders are usually tapered; ascenders are straight, and occasionally finished with a wedge or a serif to the left. Minims usually have serifs, and the downstrokes can be uneven. Latin is distinguished by the caroline forms of f, g, r, and s.
    • Summary of the characteristics of the hand: Summary of the characteristics of the hand. The a is Caroline, with a rounded bowl and bow, the former often touching the preceding letter; æ is generally uneven, sometimes dropping below the line; c is interesting, with its pointed top and angular, flat upper stroke; Lower-case d is round-backed and the same size as eth. On occasions, it has a distinctive flick to the right at the top (image 98); e is somewhat angular with an extended tongue in final position; insular f with extended midstroke; the angular g has two forms—a typically insular 5-shape, the tail beginning in the middle of the headstroke, and sometimes, a 3-shape, with the tail beginning from the right. The tail might occasionally close with a hairline stroke. Insular h has both feet to the right, though very sporadically caroline h is used with an exaggerated right foot descending below the line; the lower leg of the infrequent k is raised; l often has a serif to the left at the top; r is the insular shepherd’s crook. Long s, which flicks to the left, and high s both have an angular shoulder. Thorn has relatively large and rounded bowl, especially relative to the straight, short, ascender and descender; eth varies between the upside-down Arabic 4 shape and the large rounded bowl, with a cross-stroke that flicks distinctively to the right at the top; wynn had a large pointed bowl; x has a long, extended left foot reaching under the preceding graph; straight-limbed, dotted, y with serifs to the left of both upper limbs. Capital A is flat-topped with marked square feet; capital Æ is occasionally formed by an enlarged spurred E, uncial in shape with extended vertical serifs. Capital eth is either the rounded form (and has dotted terminals to the backward-leaning cross-stroke sometimes, e.g., image 91), or D with a line through the vertical. Capital e is uncial and has an extended tongue. Capital N has a marked mid-bar; uncial capital s is flat-topped; capital T has a curved stem.
    • Abbreviations: Tironian nota has angular tapering descender, though it sometimes finishes with a flick to the right on, or just below, the line; .
    • Punctuation: Straight macron; Punctus on the line; Punctus elevatus with a curved upper part.
    • Ligatures: Tall st ligature on many pages (but not 98r) and many of the letter forms written in a semi-current manner, giving the appearance of ligature: gg, fo, tt [there’s a lot of double of consonants here, which aren’t traditional. Makes me think of the Orrmulum]; this is never the case with minims, however, which are carefully individual.
    • Language: The scribe uses a greater number of twelfth-century spelling, departing from West Saxon features (Crawford 1922, pp. 429-436; Pope 1967, pp. 38-39; Rosier 1964, pp. 8-11)
    • Other manuscripts: This is the same hand as found in CCCC 367 --[Check].

    Hand: 2
    • Scope: major
    • Script: Anglo-Saxon Insular minuscle
    • Description: This scribe copied fols 53r-120v. The scribe uses a type of Anglo-Saxon Insular minuscle characteristic of the mid second half of the twelfth century. The aspect is upright and engrossed; the ascenders are slightly wedged. The descenders are occasionally tapered to the left. The hand extends below or above the line of writing in a proportionate way. The scribe mixes caroline and insular letter forms, and a number of gothic features are also present.
    • Summary of the characteristics of the hand: Summary of the characteristics of the hand. Caroline a with a small lobe, and pronounced hooked like straight head broken stroke; a rounded lobe of the b with wedged straight ascender; a one broken stroke caroline c; d has a thick almost upright back stroke which curves pointed to the right a small rounded lobe. Small closed e with a little tongue on the right side of the head, more evident in final position. Caroline f, with the shaft sitting on the line with a wedged foot. The scribe uses an insular form of g with a flat head and a well formed descender which curls towards the left almost into a closed loop, occasionally the closing is complete especially when this letter appears in combination with a and other minims.Gothic type of h with wedged ascender and wedged feet to the shat and the a right limb. The minims of i, m, n and u are traced singularly, regular in shape and with pronounced feet. Tall wedged ascender l, with the shaft finishing on the line with a small feet curling to the right. Rounded closed o. p and ƿ are mainly distinguishable through meaning. p has a tapered descender and a closed lobe of squarish proportions which closes past the shaft on the left. ƿ is formed with a thick tailed descender generally curving towards the left and a closed elongated lobe. Insular r with a well defined bow and a descender which curves on the left. Gothic s with a curved head-stroke and well defines feet. t with straight head stroke and straight shaft which curls to the right. y is always dotted and the descender curves to the left. æ formed by a caroline shape-like a and an e, which sits on its protruding headstroke and terminates with a tail on the top right. ð has pointed lobe, wedged ascender and tailed descender occasionally pointing to the left. þ is formed by a forty-five degree ascender and a median thick stroke across the back stroke and terminates, occassionally on both side, with a serif curving to the right and/or left. Other features include: use of capitals, but not regularly, after the Punctus. Pen ink work is regularly used to decorate Litterae Notabiliores and in particular the descender of any letter forms, including the paraph mark, may develop in ornate leaves or floreal motives.
    • Abbreviations: Abbreviations are not used regularly, a part from the use of tironian nota. Occasioanl use of crossed through þ for þæt and rare use of macron.
    • Punctuation: Punctus and paraph marks
    • Ligatures: No ligature, but interisting use of biting of letter forms in conjunction with insular g.
    • Language: The scribe uses a number of twelfth-century spelling. (Crawford 1922, pp. 429-436; Pope 1967, pp. 38-39; Rosier 1964, pp. 8-11)

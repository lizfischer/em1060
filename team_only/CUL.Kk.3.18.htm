Bede, Historia ecclesiastica

Date: s. xi2

Summary:

The Old English translation of Bede's Historia ecclesiastica gentis anglorum, written in Worcester. The manuscript contains contemporary interlineata and marginalia both in English and Latin added also in Worcester.

      Note: Book numbers in rustic capital are given as the running title at the head of the page throughout. Hand description:

    Number of hands: at least 7
    Summary: The whole of the main text was copied by a single hand, probably Hemming. Ker says that there are 'some contemporary corrections, alterations and glosses' (Ker, 1957, p. 37). These additional hands are analysed and categorised wherever possible with the very limited information, i.e., from the glosses and additions. I have identified and described three contemporary hands who added English text to this manuscript, but there are possibly more. C11 Database Project provides a useful list of glosses, additions and alterations, although I do not always agree with its attribution of hands. For example, they attribute a marginal addition in fols. 63v/15 to 'an 11th century Worcester hand'. The paleographical analysis, however, suggests that this was added by the main scribe. The cross mark in the margin, on the other hand, is similar to those used by Coleman. The C11 Database Project also attributes additions of a single letter e to Coleman, one with a question mark, and the other definitely (fols 85r/16 and 88v/2). Attributing an addition of a single letter to a certain scribe is extremely difficult. There are other added 'e'-s in a similar shape, but the C11 Database Project does not attributed them to Coleman. {{ Check the colour of ink. }}
    Methods of Correction: {{ET!! - Call this as 'Methods of Alterations'? }} Corrections and alterations appear consistently from the beginning to the end. The methods of deletion are physical erasure of letters by scratching off the surface of the parchment and expunction marks. The methods of addition are overwriting letters on the top of erasure, interlinear additions with or without caret mark, and additions in the margin. Some of them were definitely by the main scribe, some definitely by later hands, and some are not possible to attribute to a specific hand.
    Hand: main text
    • Scope: Major
    • Scribe: Hemming (?)
    • Script: English Vernacular Minuscle
    • Date: s. xi2
    • Description: This scribe copied the whole of the main text (fols 3-99: Preface, Genealogy, Table of Contents and History). Ker identifies this hand as probably Hemming, monk of Worcester (Ker, 1948, pp. 57 and 72; Ker, 1957, p. lvi), and describes it as 'a regular, stiff, and rather ugly hand which occurs also in five other Worcester manuscripts and in Harley Charter 83.' (Ker, 1957, Item 23). Ker's observation that this hand is 'ugly' is subjective, as Miller describes this as 'a neat hand' (p. xx). The script is regular and relatively round, with a broad horizontal spread.

      Rustic capitals for the incipit (fol. 8v) and book headings. Chapter numbers given throughout the text are probably by him too. The capitals have a style, and they are written in calligraphic script.

      The scribe sometimes wrote the last few words of the chapter to the right end of the first line of the next chapter in order to avoid widow. The first line of the new chapter and the last few words from the previous chapter were often marked them with a dividing line, probably by later hands, and some of them were possibly by Coleman. The dividing lines were sometimes coloured or emphasized further.

    • Summary of the characteristics of the hand:
    • Ker says that a is 'nearly caroline', but caroline a with a short and straight loop at the top occurs only occasionally. a is mostly in Insular form with a single compartment. It is slightly rounded triangular at the top;
    • æ 'with regular high e', as Ker says;
    • d is very low with a little flick, which often creates a slight concave;
    • Insular e is often horned; long tongue in final position;
    • ę is occasionally used;
    • the descender curves slightly to the left at the end; the tongue of f is longer than the shoulder and curves up very slightly at the end;
    • the headstroke of Insular g -- when there is one -- 'is often slightly concave', as Ker says. The downstroke of the letter is sometimes connected to the headstroke at the very left end, and shapes a round top;
    • Insular h sometimes have distinct serifs to the right, sometimes small feet to the right. The ascender is wedged;
    • minim sometimes have distinct serif to the right, sometimes small and flat feet;
    • p has a round lobe, and its descender finishes with a straight line serif, which turns up to the right;
    • r is in Insular form with a straight descender, and a straight right foot with a serif to the right;
    • three kinds of s: the Insular s with a straight descender and a small head. The right end of the head curves downward; Caroline long s with a head which curves gently (often finishing without curving downwards) and a descender curving to the left. The head often joins the descender at its half way down. The double ss are mostly spelt with two long ss. {{ -- judging on what I have noticed so far. I should keep my eyes open. }} ; and the long s at the end of a word sometimes has a distinct curly head;
    • t has a round back, and 'the headstroke of t is often slightly concave', as Ker says.
    • the ascender of þ is often wedged, sometimes forms a serif to the left at the top. The descender is shorter than the ascender, and straight.
    • ð has a round lobe, and its ascender goes up to the left at an angle of 45 degrees, extending much further than the left end of the lobe. The top of the ascender is straight, and the crossbar has a small serif pointing upwards to the left end and downwards to the right end.
    • y is dotted. The descender is straight at an angle of 45 degrees, and sometimes long with a small flick to the right.
    • ƿ often has a triangular lobe, with a pointed corner at the top right, but the shape of the lob is sometimes round and elongated. The lobe meets the stem below the writing line. The descender is without a serif, and sometimes curves slightly to the left and tapers;
    • ascenders are longer than descenders, except on d. Ascenders of b, h, l often have small wedges at the top;
    • descenders are relatively short and straight.
    • accents are sometimes found on vowels.
    • A used in the Incipit and Book headings is usually triangular, and has the bottom line, whereas a capital A within the text is open at the bottom. A tall Caroline a is also used within the text.
    • Æ within the text uses an open rustic A, and the head of e is very small.
    • The bottom horizontal line of rustic E is usually the shortest. The top horizontal line often has a small downward serif to the left at its end, and the middle horizontal line is sometimes wavy and the longest of the three. A tall Caroline e with a tongue to the right is used as a capital letter within the text.
    • Rustic F has a distinct diagonal head, while the back and the horizontal bar are very short. The right end of the head is higher than other letters. The capital letter F within the text, on the other hand, is tall and balanced, and it has serifs at the right ends of two horizontal lines, which are approximately the same length, and it also has a foot.
    • H in the Incipit is a Caroline h in a larger size.
    • The ascender of L in the Incipit is higher than other letters, while the horizontal bottom line is very short.
    • The V-shape part of M both in the Incipit and in the main text is on the writing line, and the final diagonal stroke curves inward and tappers upwards at the end. In the main text, uncial M is also used.
    • The final stroke of N in the Incipit extends below the writing line and tappers to the left. The diagonal stroke in the middle is sometimes at less than 15 degree. The main text has two kinds of N: the letter with the long final stroke, and a balanced letter, with distinct serifs at the top and bottom of the left descender, and the right desender with a split top.
    • S in the Incipit and Book Headings is higher than other letters, and its top lobe is larger than the bottom one.
    • The head of T in the Incipit and Book Headings is decorative. It is wavy and its right end goes upwards, and sometimes curves back to the left.
    • Abbreviations:
    • Tironian nota with straight head and straight downstroke; & is occasionally used.
    • Macron often has upward serif at the left end, and downward serif at the right end.
    • þæt with a thorn and a cross bar.
    • ƀ for 'bisscop'.
    • Punctuation: punctuation at the end of a chapter is three dots in triangle above a comma. The text is mainly punctuated by a period or a punctus above the writing line.

Decoration Description:

    Large initials; textual divisions: Book and chapter divisions are marked mostly with one-line (sometimes two-line) large initials throughout. Beginning of each book is in metallic red, and chapters begin with a coloured initials, {{ alternately metallic red and purple }} . About half of the width of a letter is in the margin, and half is indented within the main text. The very beginning of the main text is marked with a three-line capital letter 'B' (20v). For the layout of 8v, see also 5. Item (4) above. The final strokes of some of the large capitals have simple flourishes, such as small curls: 'F' (60r), 'Ƿ' (60v), 'M (61v), 'Ƿ' (79r), and so on. Some letters have more elaborate flourishes: 'O' (32r) and 'Ę' (47v); some letters are coloured in {{ xxx }} : 'Ð' (6v), 'Ð' (9v), 'Ę' (20r), 'Ð' (20v).

Additions:

    Two kinds of paragraph marks are added in the margin by a later hand. The first one consists of lines, and it looks like a combination of a letter 'E' and a long vertical line which slants to the left at the bottom end (27v, 35r, 98r, etc). The other one is in a shape of usual paragraph mark, but the top horizontal line ends with a flourish which curls upward, and the vertical line extends downwards and ends with a flourish which curls upward to the left (38v, 72r, 83v, etc). The former was probably added first, as in fol. 90r, the former is physically erased and the latter is added on the top.

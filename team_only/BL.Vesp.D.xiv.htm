Homilies, etc.

Date: s. xii med

Summary:

A broad collection of homilectic material, the bulk drawn from the first and second series of Ælfric's Catholic Homilies. Among the material not attributed to Ælfric are included homilies on Saint Neot and the Phoenix, the Old English Distichs of Cato, a collection of prognostications, and translations of a homily of Ralph d'Escures and the Elucidarium of Honorius Augustodunensis. Also present are portions of Ælfric's letters to Sigefyrð and Wulfstan. A copy of Isidore's Synonyma, among a number of Latin texts composing a second manuscript written in a continental hand of s. ix, bound together with the collection of homilies since at least Cotton's acquisition, is glossed with English word in drypoint. (by William Green)

This manuscript contains a variety of texts. Most articles are taken from Ælfric's first and second series of homilies. Arts. 1-2 are prayers added to the manuscript. It also contains the Dicts of Cato (Art. 5), Augustine, 32 is a translation of John 13:1-13, 33 is an abridged "Embassy Ralph d’Escures homily on the Virgin Mary (Art. 45) and sections from the Elucidarium of Honorius (Arts. 47-8). There are also a selection of various shorter texts.(by Orieeta) Punctus elevatus given as ';'

History:

    Origin: Written in Spain or Portugal in the middle of the 13th century (the date 1042, given in a marginal note on f. 97v, cannot be correct.)
    Provenance: The Spanish scholar Benito Arias Montano (1527-1598) has written his name on f. 97r, and may be presumed to have owned the manuscript. It came somehow into the possession of etatsråd Holger Parsberg (1636-1692), who has written his name twice, once on the front pastedown and once on f. 1r, the former dated 1680 and the latter 1682. Following Parsberg's death the manuscript was bought by etatsråd Jens Rosenkrantz (1640-1695) when Parsberg's library was auctioned off (23 October 1693).
    Acquisition: The manuscript was acquired by Árni Magnússon from the estate of Jens Rosenkrantz, presumably at auction (the auction lot number 468 is written in red chalk on the flyleaf), either in 1696 or 97.

Homilies

Date: "s.xii med" Manuscript dated by Ker, 1957 s. xii1, though probably closer to s. xiimed

Summary:

The manuscript contains seventy-three texts: homilies for Sundays and feast days in the Temporale and the second half of the Sanctorale, together with miscellaneous items at the end. The contents can be divided into five groups (Godden, 1979, p. xxxiv) arranged, for the most part, according to the church year: the homilies from the second Sunday in Epiphany (this first homily only survives as four lines) to the twenty-first Sunday after Pentecost. As the manuscript is incomplete at the beginning (having lost 44 folios) and end, it probably also contained texts for the first half of the Sanctorale originally. Sixty-three of the pieces are by Ælfric, the remainder being anonymous (with the first erased).

Hand description:

    Number of hands: 3
    Summary: There are two main hands in the manuscript, writing a very regular, angular script. Scribe A wrote pages 1-50, 203-the end. Scribe B wrote pp. 51-202. Insular forms of f and g are used, but caroline a and r. The two scribes who wrote the bulk of it show so many features in common that it appears they were trained in the same style, one to which they both attempt to adhere. A third scribe, who was also the corrector, rubricator and miniator, wrote pp. 226.27-231.28, 251.10-254.05, and, unnoticed by Ker, 1957, also wrote all of p. 233. This scribe uses insular r, f, and g. Methods of correction include expunction, interlinear insertions, overwriting, and striking through. If this scribe was senior to the other two scribes, s/he may have deliberately stayed abreast of developments in script. It might also be that Scribe C was accustomed to writing documents where less formal elements of scribe were more usual. This writing certainly suggests a mid-twelfth-century date for the manuscript as a whole since these stints are not additions to the manuscript, but form an integral part of its copying.

History:

    Origin: Almost certainly attributable to Rochester Cathedral Priory.

    Acquisition: Parker bequeathed it to Corpus Christi College in his Indenture of 1575.

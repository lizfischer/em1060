King Alfred's translation of Gregory the Great, The Pastoral Care

Date: s. xi3/4

Summary:

Incipit of the preface (Item 1; fol. 5r/3) shows that this copy is derived from one sent by Alfred to Wulfsige who was bishop of Sherborne in the last decade of s. ix and who may be identical with Wulfsige the successor of Heahstan, bishop of London, c. 900. It is followed by a metrical note (Item 2; fols 6v-7r) and by a list of the 65 chapters, numbered incorrectly I-LXIII (Item 3; fols 7r-10r). The text begins on fol. 10v (Item 4), and ends imperfectly: the chapters in the text are not numbered.

      Decoration: The first line of the text is all in metallic red, and in very large Rustic capital letters. Chapter headings in metal red and green, and new chapters begin with large initial letters written in the margin. The coloured initials are alternately red, blue, and green. Sometimes the scribe changed the line to begin the heading, but he always chose to bite into the space for the next chapter, rather than having only one or two words. There is no white space between chapters. The chapters are not numbered. The scribe sometimes began sentences with an emphasized (but not coloured) letters (for example, 52r/5).

Hand description:

    Number of hands: 1
    Summary: The whole text was copied by a single hand: a round and upright script, which share many of the principal characteristics of the Exeter type. Drage calls it as 'the most calligraphically accomplished piece of insular minuscule produced at Exeter' (p. 173). When Latin is used, the letter forms are distinctively different.
    Methods of Correction:

    Ker says 'no corrections or alterations' (p. 28), and C11 Database Project does not have a list of annotation.

    There are, however, regular interlinear additions by the main hand. For example, 62r/14: to; 64r/2: galethas; 88v/21: ⁁ man; 104v/24: ge ƿorh⁁ t; 108r/22: ⁁ un; 88v/21: nan ⁁man mid; 101r/23: ge struyne.

    Methods of cancellation include both physical erasures and expunction. For physical erasure, see for example, 46v/12: ƿsƿiðe ('s' is overwritten); 111r/11: þxlifes; 88r/20: hƿæene; 94v/01 and 03: 'tantalius' and 'syinblede'.

    Some cases of correction might be by a different hand, or the same scribe but at a later stage. For example, 85r/09: ærest eaƿfæst; 103v/21: 'þuh þurh'; 117r/03: 'þalandhand' (probably much later hand).

    Hand: 1
    • Scope: major
    • Script: English Vernacular Minuscle
    • Description: This scribe copied the whole of the main text (Preface, Metrical Note, Table of Contents and The Pastoral Care).
    • Summary of the characteristics of the hand:
    • a is in the rounded insular form;
    • Occasional high e in æ, especially before t or g;
    • d is round, almost o-like in form, with a short ascender which is flicks backward at the top. It is carefully differentiated in height from ð;
    • e is often horned to the left, with a tongue to the right;
    • the insular form of low f, its tongue on the writing line;
    • the loop of g closes in a round bowl, and the vertical stroke joins the horizontal bar at the left;
    • h with the second limb turned outwards;
    • feet of minims finish with a serif to the right;
    • the shape of ƿ is similar to that of p, but the lobe of wyn is elongated, whereas that of p is round. The ends of descenders of ƿ curve to the left, while those of p are straight and have serifs to the right;
    • insular r;
    • long s arches at the top and finishes with a serif to the left, and insular low s;
    • t has a round back with a straight head;
    • both ð and þ are used. ð is prominent and tagged to the left at the top. The crossbar sometimes has an upward hook at the left and a downward hook at the right;
    • þ has a straight descender with a serif curving on the left;
    • straight-limbed y is always dotted, the descender curves to the left ending in a flick upwards to the right;
    • the shape of ƿ is similar to that of p, but the lobe of wyn is elongated, whereas that of p is round. The ends of descenders of ƿ curve to the left, while those of p are straight and have serifs to the right;
    • ascenders are deeply split at the top;
    • the ends of descenders curve to the left;
    • c-shaped accents are often written over short syllables (ex. fols. 15, 24, 82, 83v, 85v, 90r, 90v, and so on); Acute accents are also used on the top of vowels.
    • Square Uncial letters are sometimes used at the beginning of chapters and sentences.
    • Abbreviations:
    • tironian ˥ with flat head and the descender curves to the left;
    • that with a horizontal crossing-bar with small flicks at both ends, up on the left, and down on the right;
    • þonne n is abbreviated with a distinct zig-zag shaped contraction mark;
    • macron is a straight horizontal line. for example, 'cƿˉ' (129r/03)
    • Abbreviations are heavily used when Latin is used (for example, 83v/19)
    • Punctuation:
    • hyphens to splits words at the end of line are straight horizontal lines written slightly above the level of the line. Sometimes another hyphen was added at the beginning of the next line;
    • punctus sometimes on the line, sometimes in the form of medial point;
    • a filling : --- is used at the end of chapters.
    • Ligatures:
    • æ: when e is taller, its tongue tends to continue with the following letter.
    • ct sometimes are connected, for example: 'iacincte' (fol. 126r).
    • Other manuscripts: Drage, 1978 claims that she has not yet traced this hand in another surviving Exeter manuscript. (p. 173); Corradini, 2008 tentatively ascribes this manuscript to Drage's Scribe 2 (p. 129). {{ET!! - Any veiws? }}

History:

    Origin:

    Written almost certainly at Exeter.

    Provenance:

    The text of Alfred's preface in Parker, 1574, pp. 41-44 is derived from this copy.

    Two letters from John Jewel, bishop of Salisbury, to Parker, dated in January 1568, were pasted to fol. 149v, when Ker examined this manuscript. The letters are printed in {{ Jewl's Works, ed. Jelf, VIII, 193 and 194 }} . They refer to an OE manuscript sent to Parker from Salisbury and are pr. Wanley, 1705, p. 153. The manuscript in question is almost certainly Ker's no. 87, CTC R. 5. 22 (717), fols 72-158, Pastoral Care, s. x/xi. They are now removed from fol. 149v, and pasted on a modern paper, which is inserted between fols 149 and 150. {{ Ker says 1568/9 and pasted to f. 149v. Check the ms. }}

    Wheloc notes on the front pastedown that he began to read the text on 3 September 1638 and finished it on 17 July 1639. {{ where on the front pastedown? Check the ms. }}

    Acquisition:

    It was presented to Archbishop Parker in 1568.

    Number 6 in the list of manuscripts given to Cambridge University by Archbishop Parker in 1574. Old Cambridge marks are 'D. β. 3' and '253'.

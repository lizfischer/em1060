Eadwine Psalter

Date: s.xiimed

Summary:

Eadwine's Psalter is a glossed triple Psalter from the mid-12th century containing: a Calender, triple Psalter, Canticles, two continuous Commentaries, two Prognostications and a full page image of Eadwine. There appears to be at least 13 scribes employed in the construction of this manuscript. Many of these scribes employ similar writing styles, making some sections difficult to attribute to one particular scribe, such as with the Old English gloss on Psalms 26-77 being identified to OE1 (Webber 19). Examples of the similarities in hands are explained in Teresa Webber's article in The Eadwine Psalter (Gibson).

Another issue raised with this manuscript is whether or not exemplars were used in the Old English and Anglo-Norman glosses. It is clear that the Latin gloss and commentary was attributed to the Glossa Ordinaria, which was considered "in the main stream of contemporary scholarship" (Gibson 109). The other two glosses have not been viewed in such a positive light. The Old English gloss is considered to be mainly part of two exemplars: the Vespasian and Regius Psalters. The problem this encounters is the massive corrections made by OE2 and OE4, including modernization of language and change in content as well as "sporadic changes of sources" (O'Neill 124). Lastly, the possible exemplar used for the Anglo-Norman gloss was a corrupt manuscript which was then corrected further expanding the corruptness (Markey 150). This is only one hypothesis of two expressed by Markey. Neither of these hypotheses has been fully proved, so further study in this could prove insightful. All three of these articles can also be found in The Eadwine Psalter (Gibson).

      Note: f. 4v is blank.

      Decoration: According to Pulsiano, the initial decoration of KL at the top of the calendar months do not have gold or coloured backgrounds (49). There are five colours used within the calendar: yellow ochre, green, blue, red and brown. It is also believed that the artist is the same throughout the manuscript. As for the initials of KL, the ascender is very thick with a thin serif at the top and also at the base. The base line of the L has a thin connector and widens out at the end suggesting that line was drawn towards the left. Also, each of the initials have different decoration. Most of the decoration lies in the upper regions of the ascenders.

      Decoration: There a total of 166 coloured outline drawings of the Psalms, Canticles and Creeds. There are also over 500 decorated letters found at the beginning of the three Latin texts of the Psalms. These letters are fully decorated and highlighted in gold (Heslop 25). With exception of Psalm 1 previously discussed, there are three known Illuminators responsible for the illustrations. The Mount Sion Artist is responsible for Psalm 2, which lacks a definite border (45). Psalms 3, 5, 6, and 7 were composed by the Principal Illuminator. As will be seen later, he is responsible for all but fifteen of all the illustrations and the majority of the initials and Picture Leaves (45). He follows the Utrecht Psalter more exactly than any of the other Illuminators. Also unlike his colleagues, he does not update the clothing of the figures (45). For his own personal touch, he adds a visual gloss to his illustrations, similar to the glossing provided for the text (45). Psalm 4 was added by the Corpus 4 Master. He is the only Illuminator whose work can be found in other manuscripts (47). He is very attentive to detail in the costumes as well as distinguishing varieties of hairstyles (47). His version of Utrecht's Psalm 4 illustration is quite different from the original. His alterations include, enlarging figures relative to the landscape, closer proximity of the figures, the three dogs are now a goat, pig and sheep, among many others (47-8). With the exception of twelve, the remaining illustrations were composed by the Principal Illuminator. Not found in the Eadwine's Psalter, but believed to be once a part of it, are eight pages of painted biblical narrative scenes (25). These Picture Leaves, now found in London and New York, are: New York, Pierpont Morgan Library, MS M. 724; London, British Library, MS Add. 37472; New York, Pierpont Morgan Library, MS M. 521; and London, Victoria and Albert Museum, MS 661. How this was able to be deduced was by comparing the faces found in all the manuscripts (27). As for the illuminate initials, the majority of them consist of scrollwork and foliage although some of them also contain zoomorphic and human figures. Also found at the end of various bits of text, such as folio 9v, are animal-like figures as well as heads.

      Decoration: The Eadwine portrait is the full size of the page and was added around 1170, some 20 years after the psalter's original completion. It is lavishly coloured decorated. The inscription which borders it comprises of a mixture of capitals and uncials written in red with the exception of scriptor and littera, which are written in green.

      The two waterworks drawings located on ff.284v-285r and f.286r consist of two contemporary plans of an octagonal tower which controlled the water supply to the cathedral and monastic buildings at Christ Church. The smaller drawing was drawn mainly with dark brown ink, but also included green, khaki-yellow, and orange-red (Woodan 170). Compared to the larger drawing, the small drawing is of little significance. The larger drawing provides a bird's eye view of the waterworks system and was drawn into the completed Psalter after it was bound and was turned upside down when it was rebound in the seventeenth-century (171). It was drawn primarily in ink with various other colours washed in (171.)

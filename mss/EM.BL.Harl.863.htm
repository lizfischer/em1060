Continuous gloss to the Quicumque vult written in the Leofric Psalter

Date: s. xi3/4

Summary:

The Leofric Psalter. On fol. 107 is the Quicumque vult, which has a continuous Old English gloss beginning 'sƿa hƿa sƿa wile hal ƿesan toforan eallum | þearf ys þæt healde þone fulfremedan geleafan' (Holthausen 1942-43).

Condition:

As Pulsiano notes, 'leaves show signs of wear and are generally darkened throughout, with the gutters noticeably darkened. The last leaf is dirty and worn, suggesting that the book was without a cover for a while' (1996, p. 37). Some leaves are repaired by stitching; there are also some modern repairs.

Note:

    Leaves are arranged HFHF. Part of the text (8 lines) is erased at fol. 38v.

Layout description:

  • Layout:
      • Ruled for 25 lines, single column, double bounding lines. Prickings in the outer margins. The coloured initial capital letters are place in the left double bounding lines, which is about 1 cm wide. See diagram.

Hand description:

    Number of hands: 1 in Old English
    Summary: One hand in Old English in a script contemporary with the script of the psalter, canticles and litany (Ker 1957, p. 307).
    Hand: Glosses
    • Scope: Minor
    • Script: English Vernacular Minuscle
    • Description: Fol. 107r-v.
    • Summary of the characteristics of the hand: Exeter type script, in very thin pen.
    • e is high in the combination æ.
    • g is insular with its loop either open or closed.
    • h is insular with distinct serifs at both feet.
    • The descender of long s extends below the writing line and tapers to the left.
    • ð has a tall ascender, with a serif to the left at the top, and a cross-bar has an upwards serif at the right end.
    • y is dotted, and both limbs slightly curves over to the left at the top.
    • descenders often finish tapered and bending to the left.
    • ascenders are tall and split at the end.

Decoration Description:

    • Fols 1r-6v Red, green, blue and brown ink is used for the calendar.
    • Initials and some rubrics of the psalter are alternately green, red, metallic red and blue. Some initials are very elaborate: for example, the display line to Psalm 1 is written in red and black ink; at the beginning of Psalm 51 is an incomplete drawing in lead of an oval containing a floral design.
    • The text is divided by drawings of figures.

Additions:

  • fol. 20r A small face in red ink.
  • fol. 54r A drawing in red of a figure with a crowned human head and a serpentine body.

History:

    Origin:

    Written in Exeter.

    Provenance:

    According to Lapidge, the manuscript is 'very possibly one of the two such psalters given to the Exeter chapter by Bishop Leofric in 1072' (1991, p. 74).

    Acquisition:

    Originally owned by Edward Stillingfleet (1635-99). His collection was bought by Robert Harley in 1707 (Wright 1972, pp. xxxiv, 316), then by the British Museum in 1753.
